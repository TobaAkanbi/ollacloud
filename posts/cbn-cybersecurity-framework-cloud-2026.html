What the CBN Cybersecurity Framework Means for Your Cloud Infrastructure in 2026

O
OllaCloud
·Jul 2026·9 min read

The Central Bank of Nigeria's Risk-Based Cybersecurity Framework and Guidelines for Deposit Money Banks and Payment Service Providers is not new — but its implications for cloud infrastructure are becoming harder to ignore. As more Nigerian banks and fintechs move workloads off on-premises hardware and onto cloud platforms, the framework's requirements for data localisation, third-party risk management, and incident response directly govern which cloud architectures are acceptable — and which are not.

This article is written for CIOs and Compliance Officers at Nigerian financial institutions who are evaluating, planning, or currently operating cloud infrastructure. It explains what the CBN framework actually requires, where the most common compliance gaps appear in cloud environments, and what a compliant cloud architecture looks like in practice.

New directive (June 2026): The CBN issued circular PSS/DIR/PUB/CIR/001/004, signed by Rakiya O. Yusuf, Director of the Payments System Supervision Department. The circular directs all deposit money banks, microfinance banks, mobile money operators, switching and processing companies, payment terminal service providers, payment solution service providers, super agents, and other licensed payment operators to store and manage payment transaction data generated within Nigeria on local servers.

What the CBN Cybersecurity Framework Covers

The CBN's cybersecurity framework establishes minimum standards for how deposit money banks (DMBs) and payment service providers (PSPs) must manage cybersecurity risk. It covers governance, risk management, access controls, incident response, and — critically for cloud decisions — data management and third-party service provider oversight.

The framework is not a suggestion. Compliance is mandatory for all CBN-licensed institutions, and CBN examinations include assessments of whether institutions have implemented the required controls. Non-compliance carries the risk of regulatory sanctions and, in the event of a breach, significantly increased liability.

For cloud infrastructure specifically, three areas of the framework carry the most operational weight.

1. Data Localisation and Residency Requirements

The CBN has been explicit that payment transaction data generated within Nigeria must be stored and managed on local servers. Circular PSS/DIR/PUB/CIR/001/004 (June 2026) applies to all deposit money banks, microfinance banks, mobile money operators, and licensed payment operators. The circular states that the localisation requirement must align with Nigeria's data protection laws and regulations.

In a cloud context, this means that the physical location of the data centre where your data is stored, processed, and backed up must be inside Nigeria. A cloud provider with a Lagos point of presence does not automatically satisfy this requirement if your data is replicated to overseas regions for redundancy, if your backups are stored in South Africa or Europe, or if your support team can access your environment from a foreign jurisdiction.

Key distinction: "Data processed in Nigeria" is not the same as "data hosted by a provider with a Nigerian office." The CBN's residency requirement refers to where data physically resides on storage infrastructure — not where a provider's sales team is located.

The practical implication is that institutions using overseas hyperscale cloud providers — AWS, Azure, Google Cloud — need to verify, at a technical level, that their data residency configuration actually keeps all customer data within Nigeria at all times, including in failure scenarios. For most configurations on these platforms, that guarantee is difficult to make with certainty.

2. Third-Party Risk Management

Cloud providers are third-party service providers under the CBN framework, and the institution — not the cloud provider — remains accountable for the security and availability of the services and data on that infrastructure. This creates what is effectively a pass-through liability: if your cloud provider has a security incident, you are responsible for it from a regulatory standpoint.

The framework requires institutions to conduct due diligence on cloud providers before onboarding, maintain enforceable contractual controls through Data Processing Agreements and service agreements, and have the ability to audit or obtain audit reports from their cloud providers on request.

This is where many institutions using large overseas cloud providers hit a practical obstacle. AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud do not permit customer-initiated audits of their physical infrastructure — they provide third-party audit certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001) instead. The CBN framework does not explicitly prohibit this arrangement, but the ability to demonstrate control over your cloud provider's practices becomes harder the further that provider is from direct regulatory reach in Nigeria.

What Due Diligence Should Cover

At a minimum, a CBN-compliant third-party risk assessment for a cloud provider should address:

  • Physical location of data centres and confirmation of Nigerian data residency
  • Security certifications held by the provider (ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, PCI DSS)
  • Access controls governing who — including the provider's own staff — can access your environment
  • Incident notification timelines: how quickly the provider will notify you of a breach, and whether that timeline meets CBN requirements
  • Business continuity and disaster recovery capabilities, including recovery time and recovery point objectives
  • Exit provisions: whether you can retrieve all your data and migrate away within a defined timeframe

3. Incident Response and Business Continuity

The CBN framework requires institutions to maintain documented incident response plans and to test them regularly. For cloud-hosted systems, this means your incident response plan must account for scenarios where the cloud provider is the source of the incident — not just scenarios where your own applications or users are responsible.

The framework also sets expectations for Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO) for critical systems. Core banking systems, payment processing, and customer-facing channels are typically classified as critical — meaning any cloud architecture hosting these systems must be able to demonstrate recovery capabilities that meet the institution's documented objectives.

Practical implication: Your cloud provider's standard SLA is not your RTO. The SLA governs when the provider must restore service — but your RPO is determined by how frequently your data is backed up, and your RTO is determined by how quickly you can restore from those backups and resume operations. These need to be tested, not assumed.

Where the Compliance Gaps Are Most Common

Based on the patterns that emerge when institutions evaluate their cloud posture against the CBN framework, the most frequent gaps fall into four categories.

Gap 1: Unverified data residency

The institution has enabled a "Nigeria region" or "Africa region" setting in their cloud console but has not verified at an infrastructure level that all data — including backups, logs, and replicated copies — remains inside Nigeria at all times. In many cases, default configurations on overseas providers include cross-region replication that automatically routes data to overseas servers unless explicitly disabled.

Gap 2: Insufficient contractual controls

The institution has a commercial agreement with a cloud provider but has not signed a Data Processing Agreement that includes the specific obligations required under both the CBN framework and the NDPA — including data localisation commitments, incident notification timelines, and audit rights.

Gap 3: Shared responsibility misunderstood

The institution believes the cloud provider is responsible for compliance because the provider holds certifications (ISO 27001, SOC 2). In fact, those certifications cover the provider's own infrastructure — they do not extend to the institution's configuration of that infrastructure. Misconfigured access controls, unencrypted storage buckets, or absent audit logging on the institution's side are the institution's responsibility regardless of the provider's certification status.

Gap 4: Untested recovery capabilities

The institution has documented RTO and RPO targets for critical systems but has not run a recovery test to confirm that those targets are achievable in the cloud environment as actually configured. In a CBN examination, documented targets that have never been tested are treated as assertions, not evidence.

What a CBN-Compliant Cloud Architecture Looks Like

A cloud architecture that satisfies the CBN cybersecurity framework's requirements in a Nigerian financial institution context has the following characteristics:

  • Physical data residency inside Nigeria — all compute, storage, and backup infrastructure is located in Nigerian data centres, with no automatic replication to overseas regions
  • Compute enclave isolation — workloads are isolated at a hardware level, not just a software level, so that a compromise of one tenant's environment cannot affect another's
  • Encryption at rest and in transit — AES-256 encryption for stored data and TLS for data in transit, with encryption keys under the institution's control
  • Comprehensive audit logging — all access to the environment — including by cloud provider support staff — is logged and available for review by the institution's compliance team
  • Tested recovery capabilities — documented and tested RTO/RPO that meet the institution's critical system classifications, with evidence available for examinations
  • Direct contractual accountability — a Nigerian cloud provider operating under CBN jurisdiction, with a DPA that includes explicit data localisation commitments and incident notification obligations

The Relationship Between the CBN Framework and the NDPA 2023

One of the most important things for Compliance Officers to understand in 2026 is that the CBN framework and the NDPA 2023 operate simultaneously — and satisfying one does not guarantee satisfying the other.

The NDPA, administered by the Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC), applies to any organisation that processes personal data of Nigerian residents — which includes every bank and fintech. It imposes data residency requirements through Section 43, which restricts the cross-border transfer of personal data, and requires data controllers to implement appropriate technical safeguards.

The CBN framework, administered by the Central Bank of Nigeria, applies specifically to licensed financial institutions and payment service providers. It imposes additional sector-specific requirements around cybersecurity governance, third-party risk, and system resilience that go beyond the NDPA's general framework.

In practical terms, this means an institution whose cloud architecture satisfies CBN data residency requirements will likely also satisfy the NDPA's cross-border transfer restrictions — because physically keeping data inside Nigeria addresses both. But the reverse is not guaranteed: an institution that has addressed NDPA compliance at a policy level (through standard contractual clauses, for example) may still have unaddressed CBN gaps around third-party risk governance and recovery testing.

Five Steps for CIOs and Compliance Officers in 2026

  1. Verify actual data residency, not assumed data residency. Pull a configuration audit of your current cloud environment and confirm — at an infrastructure level — that no customer data is stored, processed, or replicated outside Nigeria. Do not rely on a provider's marketing claims or regional pricing tiers as evidence.
  2. Review your Data Processing Agreements. Confirm that your DPAs with cloud providers include explicit data localisation commitments, incident notification timelines that meet CBN requirements, and audit rights. If they do not, update them before your next examination cycle.
  3. Conduct a third-party risk assessment on your cloud providers. Document it formally. The assessment should cover the items listed above — physical location, certifications, access controls, incident notification, BCP/DR, and exit provisions.
  4. Run a recovery test. Take your documented RTO and RPO targets for critical systems and test them against your actual cloud configuration. Record the results. If your actual recovery time does not meet your documented target, address the gap before it is raised in an examination.
  5. Evaluate whether your current cloud provider can give you what the framework requires. If your current provider cannot provide Nigerian data residency with certainty, cannot offer direct audit access, or cannot give you the contractual controls the framework demands, that is a risk that needs to be managed — either by remediating the gaps in your current arrangement or by migrating to a provider that can satisfy the requirements from the outset.
