NDPA 2023 and Cloud Compliance: What Nigerian Banks Must Know

O
OllaCloud Team
· Jul 2026 · 8 min read

The Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) 2023 is the most significant data governance legislation Nigeria has ever enacted. For banks, microfinance institutions, fintechs, and any organisation that processes personal data — which is essentially every business in the financial sector — it changes the ground rules for cloud infrastructure. Not because the cloud is banned, but because the wrong cloud is now a regulatory liability.

This guide breaks down exactly what the NDPA requires, how it interacts with existing CBN data residency directives, what it means for your cloud strategy, and how to achieve full compliance without sacrificing the performance and agility that cloud infrastructure should deliver.

What Is the NDPA 2023?

The Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 was signed into law in June 2023, replacing the Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR) of 2019 as Nigeria's primary data protection framework. It establishes a comprehensive set of rights for data subjects — Nigerian citizens and residents whose personal data is collected and processed — and a corresponding set of obligations for data controllers and processors.

The Act is administered by the Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC), which has the authority to investigate breaches, issue enforcement notices, and impose significant financial penalties for violations.

For the financial sector specifically, the NDPA operates alongside — not instead of — CBN data governance frameworks. Both apply simultaneously, and compliance with one does not guarantee compliance with the other.

The Three NDPA Requirements That Directly Affect Cloud Infrastructure

1. Data Residency and Cross-Border Transfer Restrictions

The NDPA restricts the transfer of personal data outside Nigeria unless specific conditions are met. These conditions include: the destination country has an adequate level of data protection, the data subject has given explicit consent, or standard contractual clauses approved by the NDPC are in place.

In practice, this means that any cloud provider whose data centres are located outside Nigeria — AWS in Europe, Azure in South Africa, Google Cloud globally — creates an automatic cross-border transfer risk for every piece of personal data you store or process on their platform. Even if a provider has a regional point of presence in Lagos, that is not the same as data residency: data may still be replicated, backed up, or processed in overseas regions.

Key point: The NDPA does not prohibit cloud computing. It prohibits the uncontrolled transfer of personal data outside Nigeria without the appropriate legal basis. The simplest way to eliminate this risk entirely is to use cloud infrastructure that physically resides inside Nigeria.

2. Data Controller Accountability

Under the NDPA, data controllers — the banks and fintechs that determine why and how personal data is processed — are responsible for ensuring that their data processors (including cloud providers) process data in compliance with the Act. This means your cloud provider's compliance posture becomes your compliance posture.

If your cloud provider cannot demonstrate where your data is processed, who has access to it, and how it is protected, you are exposed. A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with a foreign cloud provider does not override the NDPA's residency requirements — it simply documents an arrangement that may still be non-compliant.

3. Security and Technical Safeguards

The NDPA requires data controllers and processors to implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to protect personal data against unauthorised access, loss, or destruction. For cloud infrastructure, this translates directly into requirements for encryption at rest and in transit, access controls, audit logging, and the ability to demonstrate these safeguards to the NDPC on request.

How CBN Data Residency Rules Layer on Top

The Central Bank of Nigeria's Risk-Based Cybersecurity Framework for Financial Institutions pre-dates the NDPA but reinforces it. The CBN framework requires that financial institutions demonstrate — through documented evidence — where their data resides and how it is protected.

In June 2026, the CBN went further. Circular PSS/DIR/PUB/CIR/001/004, signed by Rakiya O. Yusuf, Director of the Payments System Supervision Department, directed all deposit money banks, microfinance banks, mobile money operators, switching and processing companies, payment terminal service providers, payment solution service providers, super agents, and other licensed payment operators to store and manage payment transaction data generated within Nigeria on local servers.

This means Nigerian banks face a dual compliance obligation: NDPA for all personal data, and CBN requirements for payment transaction data specifically. A bank using a cloud provider with no Nigerian data centre cannot satisfy either obligation, regardless of what their contract says.

The Cloud Compliance Gap Most Nigerian Banks Are Currently Exposed To

Despite clear regulatory requirements, a significant number of Nigerian financial institutions continue to run workloads on overseas cloud providers. The reasons are understandable: the global hyperscalers offer mature tooling, large ecosystems, and familiar interfaces. The compliance risk has been treated as manageable because enforcement was historically light.

That is changing. The NDPC has moved from establishing itself to actively enforcing the Act. CBN examinations increasingly include questions about data residency. And reputational risk is growing: enterprise customers, particularly in telecoms, insurance, and government, now require their financial services partners to demonstrate data sovereignty as part of procurement due diligence.

The practical risk: A bank that cannot answer "where exactly is your customer data stored?" with a provable, Nigerian-domiciled answer is exposed — to NDPC investigation, CBN examination findings, and the reputational damage of a data residency breach becoming public.

What NDPA-Compliant Cloud Infrastructure Looks Like

NDPA compliance for cloud infrastructure requires three things to be true simultaneously:

  • Data physically resides in Nigeria — not in a foreign data centre, not replicated to an overseas backup region, not processed by systems outside Nigerian jurisdiction.
  • The cloud provider cannot access your data — encryption, access controls, and tenant isolation must prevent the provider's administrators from viewing customer data, even in a support context.
  • You can prove it — audit logs, architecture diagrams, and documented data flows must be available to demonstrate residency and protection to the NDPC or CBN on request.

A shared public cloud environment hosted overseas satisfies none of these. A shared public cloud environment hosted in Nigeria satisfies the first but not necessarily the second or third, depending on the provider's architecture. A private cloud appliance — dedicated hardware inside your own facility, running cloud software under your governance — satisfies all three.

Practical Steps for Nigerian Banks to Achieve Cloud Compliance

  1. Audit your current cloud footprint — map every system that processes personal or financial data and identify where that data is physically stored. If you cannot answer this question with confidence, you have a compliance gap.
  2. Classify your data — distinguish between personal data (NDPA applies), financial data (CBN applies), and non-regulated data. This classification determines which workloads must stay in Nigeria and which may extend to other environments.
  3. Evaluate your cloud providers — demand documented proof of data residency from every cloud provider you use. A contract clause is not proof. A data centre address and architecture diagram is.
  4. Move regulated workloads to compliant infrastructure — core banking systems, customer databases, payment processing, and KYC records must run on infrastructure that physically resides in Nigeria under your governance.
  5. Establish a Data Processing Agreement with every cloud provider — the NDPA requires a written DPA with all processors. Ensure it explicitly addresses residency, access controls, and breach notification obligations.
